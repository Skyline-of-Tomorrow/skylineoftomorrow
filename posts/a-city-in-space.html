A City in Space

By: Matthew Javier Gaughan
August 29th, 2020



My goal in life is to build a city in space.

Without context, this statement is absurd. It feels like a quote from a mediocre Bond villain.

But to me, a space city is a metaphor for a futurist vision of a city where technology and planning innovation makes possible a reality of material justice.

The greatest injustices are realized materially. Material injustices occur when as a direct result of disparities in political and economic power, material conditions surrounding someone’s life and their survival are of varying qualities, to the point that lower class status results in conditions threatening one’s ability to exist as a human and pursue fulfilling goals. Material injustice is when, due to disparities in income, one must live in an older building not constructed up to modern codes, which floods during the spring, causing damage to a persons’ home and incurring random costs which set back one’s ability to pursue their goals. Material injustice is when due to racial and class segregation one must live in an area where the nearest grocery store is over 40 minutes’ drive away, resulting in health disparities due to poor food quality in the vicinity, which in turn affect one’s ability to live life on their own terms. Material injustice is when immigrant communities across Europe move into ghettos which lack heating and electricity, affecting education and causing health issues, forcing a cycle of perpetual poverty.

The centrality of material injustice to the perpetuation of cycles of injustice does not deny its systemic nature. The carceral system and obstacles inherent within systems such as higher education and privatized medicine actively work to maintain disparities in power by relegating people to classes where material conditions perpetuate these cycles. However, once these systemic apparatuses are dismantled the question of the material reality of the built environment remains. Attempting to facilitate material progress through technological innovation has rightfully come under criticism in the past. Scientific progress has frequently been used as a cover or justification for human rights abuses. Critics have pointed out that the assumptions that underly certain scientific experimentation have derived from racist knowledge systems. Valid criticism has also pointed out how historic advancements in science have ignored ethical considerations and failed to account for the negative externalities of experimental results. In the words of fictional mathematician Ian Malcolm, “Your scientists were so preoccupied with whether or not they could, they didn’t stop to think if they should.”

These criticisms should be understood not as anti-science, but as constructive wariness towards a discipline that too frequently has furthered the interests of powerful parties against the little man. To frame these criticisms as anti-science is dangerous as it would abandon the terrain of technical expertise to elite interests vested in maintaining material injustice. In a system constructed with barriers to keep the lower class from achieving the technical expertise necessary to make themselves independent of elite interests, it is imperative that technical expertise not be abandoned in favor of more theoretical and politically focused means of resistance to material injustice.

I believe the solution only partly lies in dismantling the epistemic origins of certain scientific pretenses. To entirely leave the discussion over the moral and political implications of technological innovation to the realm of moral philosophers and social theorists would be irresponsible as it would abandon the obligation we have to use technological innovation towards a humanitarian end and resolve instances of material suffering. I believe that instead of an approach towards technological innovation involving solely a dissection of the origins of modern innovation, it is important that advancements be made towards a revolutionary humanitarian technology.

Revolutionary humanitarian technology is a field established in contrast to mainstream portrayals of technological advancement. In contrast to entrepreneurial technology like SpaceX, Tesla, Google Glass, or iPhones, revolutionary humanitarian technology aims to develop technological innovations to resolve immediately pressing material injustices without the end goal of profit. It aims to advance technology for the immediate use by communities in need. It reimagines scientific advancement as the procurement of new tools to allow people to live outside of material injustice and pursue a fulfilling life.

An area of exploration for the use of revolutionary humanitarian technology is within the discipline of civil engineering and architecture. The built environment can have a direct effect on breaking the cycle of material injustice. Building expertise within communities to advance technological innovation is imperative in a world where political systems fail daily. When lead pipes cannot be replaced due to bureaucratic barriers, a revolutionary approach could be to explore using innovations by technical experts within a community to efficiently filter and purify the water. When environmental destruction could cause desertification within a community leaving residents without necessary resources for survival, innovations in recycling technology and sustainable materials that lead to the development of a circular economy could prevent further ecological harm. Materials innovations could help conserve energy through capturing heat within buildings, sustainable energy could eliminate greenhouse gas emissions preventing the worst effects of climate change, and innovative water capture mechanisms could provide communities in arid regions with sustainable local water sources. These technological advancements fall under the umbrella of revolutionary humanitarian technology and offer a realm of possibility for scientific progress outside of fascist or corporate interests.

These advancements are what I am talking about when I reference the idea of building a city in space. A city in space is a vision of a world where material injustice is resolved via not only the destruction of political systems of injustice, but also through the incorporate of technological innovations in our day to day life which give people access to clean water, sustainable energy, and sustainable materials, allowing all of us to pursue our own fulfilling goals.

Futurism can be revolutionary. To imagine a future that resolves the injustices of the present is the hopeful vision that should drive humanitarian action. However, it is one thing to just picture this future. To act and move in the right direction, there must be a plan. A plan that is actionable will inevitably draw upon technical expertise and engineering.

It is with this goal in mind that I have decided to set up this website, The Skyline of Tomorrow. I aim to explore, through writing and technical modeling, new advancements in technology and their potential humanitarian applications in the field of urbanism. I hope to use my background in material science and sustainable urbanism to create a hyper-realistic vision of a possible future. A future where innovations in technology can be applied to alleviate material injustice and achieve new urban production systems that are not geared towards promoting elite power.
