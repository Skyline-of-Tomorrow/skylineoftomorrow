A Spatial Analysis of Alternative Fuel Sources in Chicago

By: Matthew Javier Gaughan
August 13th, 2020

Transitioning away from fossil fuels is integral to creating a more environmentally sustainable future. Although implementing mass public transit is the most efficient way to ensure a reduction in greenhouse gas emissions, transitioning automobiles away from traditional gasoline can serve as an intermediate step towards reducing overall fossil fuel consumption. Assessing the location of alternative fuel sources in relation to various parts of the city could serve to better identify patterns emerging in who gets to participate in sustainable environmental practices. This report looks at the locations of alternative fuel sources throughout Chicago. The three measurements used to analyze alternative fuel access were the locations of Alternative Fuel Sources and the variations in permission to access the Alternative Fuel Sources (Private versus Public access). These indicators were then compared against the variations in per capita income throughout Chicago. All data was obtained via the Chicago Data Portal and the Chicago Health Atlas.

A first glance of alternative vehicle fuel sources throughout Chicago will reveal an easy to spot spatial dependence of alternative fuel sources (AFS) [Fig 1]. There tends to be a concentration of alternative fuel throughout the loop and a greater number of AFS sites on the north side than on the south side; however, the variation in degree of access to AFS sites outside the loop is not as apparent, such that visually it is hard to determine if there is a greater degree of access to AFS sites in Rogers Park than in McKinley Park. Through a further analysis of variables it becomes more obvious the disparity in access to AFS sites throughout the south and west side of Chicago, as access to AFS sites in certain south and west side community areas is limited entirely to private and government-only AFS sites [Fig 2]. The disparity in access is less obvious when AFS sites are measured through a point in grid density analysis as communities throughout the north and south side have comparable site density, contrasted with the high density of site locations in the loop and near west loop [Fig 3]. The point in grid analysis uses a spatial unit too small to really identify the distinction in density between out of loop communities. At larger grid units the ability to draw conclusive data about AFS site distribution is made more difficult as the scale becomes too large. A Kernel Density Estimate helps to better visualize the degree of density variation between communities outside of the Loop [Fig 4]. It reveals the greater degree of density on the North side versus the south side. The weighted values assigned to AFS sites in the KDE analysis creates a more realistic analysis as AFS sites will inevitably be rarer than regular gas stations in the current economy and thus AFS sites will pull in drivers from over a greater radius than that of traditional gas stations, thus the weighted values. It becomes more accurate to use a KDE analysis as it indicates the degree of separation between successive AFS sites versus in the point in grid analysis which isolates AFS sites to quadrants.

To provide context for the disparate levels of access to AFS sites throughout Chicago a chloropleth map of per capita income throughout Chicago neighborhoods was compared to the prior point data analyses of AFS site density throughout the city [Fig 1]. Per capita income was used as an indicator of economic disparities as it was assumed that higher levels of per capita income could correlate with better qualities of living and increased political connection as communities with easily accessible aldermen tend to receive greater attention in Chicago, resulting in enhanced qualities of living and access to beneficial services which can contribute ultimately to higher per capita incomes. Per capita income was evaluated instead of gross income to control for family size and distribution of wealth over larger numbers of people. Alternative variables for assessing political power, economic power, class status, and quality of life exist and can better control for confounding variables to draw more conclusive results, but I felt comparing AFS sites with per capita income could begin to create a picture that lead to further questioning. The north side and areas near to the loop, especially the near north side and near west loop, have a higher per capita income than most of Chicago, especially in contrast with the south and west side of Chicago [Fig 1]. The per capita income levels helped to indicate the degree of economic disparity throughout Chicago and made apparent a spatial correlation between increased levels of alternative fuel access and increased per capita income. Although it becomes a possibility that population density may confound a causative conclusion. The Near West Loop neighborhood was most interesting to analyze as visually it appeared to be a point where the spatial density of the Loopâ€™s AFS sites begins to taper off to more match the density (as defined by the point in grid analysis) of the north and south side [Fig 5]. However, upon using a KDE analysis it became obvious that the Near West Loop had a level of access to AFS sites comparable to that of the Loop, where most sites were located [Fig 6]. Conclusions cannot be drawn specifically attributing the concentration of AFS sites to the higher income level of the Near West Loop neighborhood as confounding factors such as the fact that the loop is a large tourism and commercial area in Chicago could explain the high density of alternative fuel sites. As well neighborhoods of comparable per capita income as the Near West Loop such as Edison Park and Mount Greenwood did not have nearly the density of alternative fuel sites as the Near West Loop, suggesting other explanations for alternative fuel site locations [Fig. 1 & 2]. Proximity to the loop may better explain the increasing density of AFS sites in certain neighborhoods. However, this would fail to explain the northward skew of the AFS site density distribution as apparent in the KDE map [Fig. 4]. It should be noted that visually the neighborhoods with higher per capita income appear to have significantly more AFS sites or at least are in closer access to an AFS site, emphasized by the gap in access depicted in the KDE analysis between neighborhoods such as Brighton Park and Englewood [Fig. 4]. Despite closer proximity to the loop, Englewood and neighboring areas such as Gage Park and Chicago Lawn lack access to alternative fuel sites in comparison to neighborhoods such as Garfield Ridge or Uptown, which have a higher density of AFS sites as indicated by the KDE map [Fig. 1, 2, & 4]. It is not conclusive to suggest per capita income as the explanatory factor for the distribution of AFS sites, as there could be other variables, but it is significant to note the density patterns in the maps below as the effects of climate change are felt a disparate levels between communities, and the distribution of sustainable innovations and disaster prevention is unequal and frequently leaves under-served communities at risk.
