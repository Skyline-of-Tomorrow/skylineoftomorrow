Pandemics and Polymers: The Future of Urban Sanitation

By: Matthew Javier Gaughan
May 7th, 2020

The future of the Earth’s population is one of increased urbanization. It is predicted that by 2050 approximately 68% of people on earth will live in urban areas. This trend in population movement will be accompanied by a trend in development as cities expand systems, build new housing, and create new neighborhoods. This trend will also bring about a new slate of challenges for urban infrastructure and planning. One of these challenges is a predicted increase in the frequency of global pandemics.

Increasing population density contributes to a faster rate of pandemic disease spread and a higher rate of pathogen mutation, due to increased rates of transmission between host vectors. Combine this risk with the fact that population density increase outpaces the creation of necessary new sanitation and healthcare systems, and it creates the conditions for an increase in pandemics in the future.

While advances in biotechnology hope to devise cures in response to disease, it will only go so far to mitigate the effects of future pandemics. Knowing that pandemics will occur with more frequency implies a need to improve pandemic prevention systems. The future of urban planning must incorporate changes in preventative sanitation and healthcare systems.

The field of urban planning traces its origins to the development of sanitation infrastructure to respond to epidemics occurring throughout the 17th century. It was during a series of Cholera outbreaks in London during the 1830s-1850s that the “filth theory” of disease spread gained peak popularity. The filth theory posited that disease tended to emerge and spread fastest in the presence of dense amounts of pollution, sewage, and filth. Preventing the accumulation of filth in the name of stopping the spread of disease served as the foundational principle for the creation of early sanitation systems. This ultimately necessitated urban planning. Prior to the sanitation revolution most urban planning was privatized and random. Buildings would arise per the dictates of those with the money to build. Most sanitation systems were private at the time and uncoordinated as a result. The sanitation revolution encouraged the creation of building codes to coordinate systems, the creation of survey planning to improve system efficiency, and the implementation of water-carriage sewerage. Sanitary reformers functioned as de facto urban planners at a time when the urban planning profession did not exist.

The sanitation revolution helped prevent further epidemics. Water-borne pandemics and epidemics are rare in urban environments with high quality sanitation systems. Since the advent of the sanitation revolution changes have taken place in our modern sanitation systems. Technology has been updated based on newer studies and more comprehensive survey data has enabled better planning. One way in which modern innovation can help to update existing sanitation systems is through the incorporation of advanced materials.

The SARS-CoV2 Pandemic, which causes COVID-19, has shown how rapidly contagious disease can spread within a globalized population. Early precautions against the viral spread focused primarily on reducing contact with the virus via hand washing and wiping down surfaces. This was meant to target one of the primary modes of viral transmission in modern society: contact with fomites, which are surfaces carrying live contagious microbes. A sanitation system preparing for the future of pandemics could focus on redesigning fomites to minimize contagion exposure. One method of redesigning surfaces could be to modify their material composition to enhance antimicrobial properties, thus making surfaces de facto self-cleaning and preventing fomite infection.

The use of antimicrobial materials is not new. Around the same time as the first sanitation systems were created, physician Victor Burq was studying the mysteriously low rate of cholera infection among copper miners. Burq’s conclusion was that copper had a preventative effect on the spread of cholera. Incorporating antimicrobial materials, such as copper, could significantly reduce fomite infection. A clinical trial run across three hospitals determined that when enveloping the most contaminated surfaces closest to a patient in copper, the microbial presence on these surfaces was reduced 83% resulting in a 58% reduction in Healthcare Associated Infections (HAIs).

Thus, the current challenge, to integrate antimicrobial materials into existing construction and design frameworks, advancing these production models to enable a sanitation system for tomorrow.

Unfortunately, the use of copper in construction fell out of style, with cheaper and more aesthetically pleasing plastic and steel incorporated into modern design. Plastic and steel, however, lack the antimicrobial properties of copper. When it comes to coronaviruses studies have indicated copper’s effectiveness at killing the virus in comparison to other materials. Whereas SARS-CoV2 can last several days on plastic and stainless steel and a full day on cardboard, when on copper, the virus only lasts four hours maximum. Thus, the current challenge, to integrate antimicrobial materials into existing construction and design frameworks, advancing these production models to enable a sanitation system for tomorrow.

Innovations in polymer science allow for the creation of more efficient antimicrobial materials, and scientists are developing innovative means of implementing these materials into existing systems. New materials incorporating biocidal agents into homopolymers (polymers composed of one type of monomer), copolymers (polymers composed of multiple types of monomers), and nanocomposites (materials composed of multiple polymers) can emphasize the antimicrobial properties while varying mechanical properties, allowing for more diverse applications.

Antimicrobial polymers are especially useful since they are adaptable to various surfaces with a range of mechanical properties. They are very industry applicable as they can be fabricated using various techniques. One fabrication technique involves adding a biocidal agent after processing of the polymer. Another technique involves adding a biocidal agent to the polymer during processing, which can itself be done through multiple methods.

The first method is through the polymerizing or copolymerizing of a biocidal monomer. Acrylic types of pharmaceutical drugs are frequently used as monomer units for this method. Polymerizing the acrylic type of the common herbicide, Dichlorophenol, researchers developed homopolymers and co-polymers of 2,4-dichlorophenyl acrylate (2,4-DMA), which resulted in an 72-80% reduction in bacterial growth and 72-82% reduction in fungal and yeast growth. Copolymerization of Quaternary monomers (QAMA) with 2-hydroxyethyl methacrylate, a monomer known for easy polymerization, resulted in 100% disinfection in under 10 minutes when the QAMA content in the copolymer was over 40%.

The second method is through covalently bonding antimicrobial functional groups onto polymers through a process known as grafting. Quaternary ammonium compounds are disinfectant agents which can be grafted onto polymers, enabling antimicrobial properties. When Quaternary ammonium and Quaternary phosphonium groups were grafted onto polymers, they allowed for these polymers to have antimicrobial properties that resulted in 100% surface disinfection in under 24 hours. Silver ions can also be grafted onto polymer fibers, resulting in enhanced antimicrobial activity. Silver, along with the polymer Chitosan, has been explored in the context of creating organic antimicrobial polymers for use in medicine and food preservation, due to their non-toxic nature. Chitosan is a naturally occurring polymer derived from shrimp and crab shells, which can be chemically bound with other polymers, via process known as crosslinking, to form nanocomposites for use in antimicrobial textiles. Chitosan, when modified, can kill up to 100% of bacterial and fungal presence on surfaces in under 24 hours at concentrations as low as 10 milligrams per milliliter.

Scientists aspire to achieve certain characteristics when developing antimicrobial polymers that make industrial production easier while maintaining practical application. Polymers should be able to be easily and inexpensively synthesized. They should remain stable over time and varying temperatures. If the polymer is being created for use in liquids it should not be soluble in the target liquids. The polymer should be non-toxic and lack toxic byproducts to allow for human handling. Ideally the polymer can regenerate itself and maintain its antimicrobial properties upon loss of activity. Most importantly, a good antimicrobial polymer should be broadly and quickly biocidal, such that it can kill a wide range of pathogenic microorganisms within a short time. If a polymer only works when exposed to a pathogenic microorganism for several days, then it is not very practically applicable.

Mechanical and chemical properties of polymers vary depending on their composition and synthesis, which makes them widely applicable across industries. Antimicrobial polymers specifically are relevant in industries such as water treatment, surface disinfectants, biomedical implants, food preservation, and the development of antimicrobial textiles. In each industry, polymer-based sterilization has several advantages over current sterilization practices. One major advantage, common between industries, is that antimicrobial polymers allow for self-sterilizing materials, which enhances their durability and longevity. Antimicrobial polymers can also be non-toxic and can avoid producing toxic byproducts, resolving an issue with several current disinfectants and water treatment procedures which leave behind toxic residue.

Clothes could be made of antimicrobial textiles, where antimicrobial polymers might be crosslinked with textile fibers to enable clothing that does not decay. Drinking water could be purified through a simple filter composed entirely of a porous antimicrobial polymer film. Handrail and train platform surfaces could be layered with antimicrobial nanocomposite films or sprayed with a layer of water insoluble antimicrobial polymers, effectively reducing the presence of pathogenic microorganisms across the surface of public spaces. Antimicrobial polymers could also enable long-term food preservation through a sustained release of biocidal agents from grafted functional groups. These innovations would radically change the appearance of the future.

Researchers continue to push the bounds of knowledge in the field of antimicrobial polymers, developing new biocidal compounds to enable novel mechanical properties. A topic requiring further research is the use of antimicrobial polymers in soil sterilization. This could help change the face of urban gardening and farming in general, preventing crop blights and increasing food yield.

At a further point in the future, antimicrobial polymers could be the key to ending contagious disease as it is currently known.

The future of sanitation lies in the ability of human systems to directly target pathogenic microbes and prevent their spread. Developing smart sanitation creates a significant means of preventing pandemics, while also providing benefits in the areas of food and textile preservation, water treatment and filtration, and biomedical implants. Research and development of antimicrobial polymers helps to resolve problems in contemporary sanitation systems, creating novel materials with vast applicability.
